The Problem

You didn't apply for a technology leadership role. It found you.

Maybe the company grew fast and someone decided you were the person to figure it out. Maybe there was a crisis — a vendor failure, a security incident, a system that went down at the worst possible time — and suddenly you were in charge of making sure it never happened again.

Now you're sitting in meetings where people are throwing around acronyms, recommending platforms, asking for budget sign-off on things you're not sure you actually need. And somewhere in the back of your mind is a quiet fear:

What if I make the wrong call? What if I'm wasting money on the wrong things? What if I'm missing something that blows up later?

That's not incompetence. That's what happens when responsibility outpaces experience. It happens to good leaders all the time.

The problem isn't that you're not smart enough for this. The problem is you've never had a framework — a clear, structured way to assess where things actually stand, cut through the noise, and lead with confidence instead of anxiety.

That's what the Technology Clarity Playbook is.

How It Works

This isn't a course you watch and forget. It's a working session.

1

Run the Assessment (60–90 min)

Go through the Technology Clarity Assessment for your organization. Answer the questions honestly. The framework does the heavy lifting — you just have to show up with what you know.

2

Build Your Priority Matrix (30–45 min)

Take your assessment results and plug them into the Priority Matrix. You'll see, in plain terms, what actually matters right now versus what can wait.

3

Create Your Communication Plan (30 min)

Use the Leader's Communication Framework to document how you'll talk about your technology priorities — to leadership, to your team, to vendors.

4

Identify Your Risks (15–20 min)

Run the Risk Snapshot. Name your top 3. Now you're not just reacting to fires — you're anticipating them.

The result:

In one afternoon, you have a clear picture of where things stand, a ranked list of priorities, and the language to lead confidently in every technology conversation going forward.

The Story Behind This

From $100M to $800M — and the IT leader who had to keep up.

A few years ago, I got a call from a man I'll call Mike.

Mike was the IT person at an HVAC company that had grown from $100M to $800M in revenue — fast. The kind of growth that changes everything: new locations, new systems, new staff, constant pressure to keep things running while the business scaled underneath him.

He was good at what he did. But the company had outgrown what "good at what he did" used to mean. He was now managing vendors he didn't fully understand, making budget decisions he wasn't trained for, and leading a small team that was looking to him for direction he wasn't sure he could give.

Then came the breach.

Not catastrophic. But real. And suddenly Mike wasn't just the IT guy anymore — he was the person who had to stand in front of leadership and explain what happened, what it would cost, and what they were going to do about it.

He didn't have a framework. He had knowledge, instincts, and a lot of stress.

That's when we started working together. Over the next several months, I helped Mike build the clarity he needed — not by making him into a technical expert overnight, but by giving him a structured way to assess what he had, prioritize what mattered, and communicate with confidence.

He started showing up differently in leadership meetings. He stopped apologizing for not knowing things and started asking the right questions. He built a team, made decisions that held up, and earned the trust of people above him who'd been skeptical.

A year later, he was promoted to IT Director, managing 13 staff across multiple locations.

The technology didn't change that much. He changed.

That's what clarity does.

The Technology Clarity Playbook is the framework I built from that engagement — and from dozens like it. It's not theoretical. It comes from sitting across from leaders who were in over their heads, helping them find solid ground.

What Changes After

Here's what looks different on the other side of the Playbook.

  • You walk into technology conversations with a framework, not a feeling. No more nodding along hoping nobody asks a hard question. You know what to ask, what to look for, and how to respond.
  • You know what to spend money on — and what to stop. Technology budgets are full of decisions that made sense once. The Playbook helps you see which ones still do.
  • You lead your IT team instead of being led by them. When you can articulate priorities clearly, your team follows. When you can't, they fill the vacuum.
  • You stop reacting and start steering. Fires still happen. But you see them coming more often, and when they don't — you know how to respond without panic.
  • Your leadership is taken more seriously. Not because you suddenly know everything. Because you know what matters — and that's what leadership actually requires.

FAQ

Common questions.

I'm not technical at all. Will this be over my head?

No. The Playbook is specifically designed for leaders who came to technology through business — not engineering. If you can run a P&L or lead a team, you can work through this.

How long does this actually take?

The core work — assessment, priority matrix, risk snapshot, communication plan — takes most people 3–4 hours. Video walkthroughs are about 90 minutes total. You can do it in one focused afternoon, or spread it across a week.

My situation is pretty unique. Will this apply to my company?

The framework is industry-agnostic. I've used variations of this with manufacturing companies, service businesses, healthcare organizations, and professional services firms. If you have technology and a team, this applies.

What if I buy it and it doesn't work for me?

If you go through the Playbook and feel like it wasn't worth it, email me within 30 days and I'll refund you. No hoops. I'd rather you tell me it didn't land than walk away feeling burned.

How is this different from hiring a consultant?

A consultant does the assessment for you and hands you a report. That's valuable — but it doesn't build your capability. The Playbook puts you in the driver's seat. You learn to think through technology decisions yourself.

I already have an IT team. Why do I need this?

Because leading an IT team and being led by your IT team are two very different things. Most non-technical leaders default to trusting whatever their IT team recommends because they don't have a way to evaluate it. The Playbook gives you that.

Is this a course or a tool?

Both. It's structured like a course — there's an order, there are concepts to absorb. But the output is a real artifact: a completed assessment, a live priority list, a documented risk snapshot, communication templates. You don't just learn something. You produce something.
